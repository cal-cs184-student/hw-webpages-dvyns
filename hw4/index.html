Part 1: Masses and springs

1. Without any shearing constraints (structural + bending only):

With shearing disabled, the wireframe displays a clean orthogonal grid of squares. Springs only connect each point mass to its direct horizontal and vertical neighbors, so each cell appears as a plain rectangle with no diagonal lines crossing it.

2. With only shearing constraints:

With only shearing enabled, the horizontal and vertical structural edges disappear entirely. What remains is a rotated diamond lattice, only the diagonal springs are visible, connecting each mass to its upper-left and upper-right neighbors. The cloth appears as a grid of diamond shapes tilted 45°.

3. With all constraints:

With all three constraint types enabled, the wireframe shows the densest structure: the orthogonal structural grid is overlaid with shearing diagonals, subdividing every square cell into triangles. The bending springs (connecting masses two apart) are also present but visually overlap with the structural springs and are difficult to distinguish separately.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Effects of Changing ks (Spring Constant)

With a very low ks (50 N/m), the springs offer little resistance to deformation. As seen in the ks50 image, the cloth develops pronounced horizontal ripples and folds along the upper portion of the cloth, the surface buckles under its own weight and never fully smooths out even at rest.

With a very high ks (50000 N/m), the springs strongly resist deformation and the cloth hangs much more rigidly. As seen in the ks50000 image, the top edge forms only a single clean shallow curve with no wrinkling, and the overall surface is nearly flat, behaving more like a stiff sheet than a soft fabric.

Effects of Changing Density

With low density (1 g/cm²), the cloth is very light and gravity has minimal effect on it. The cloth sags only slightly, hanging with a shallow smooth curve at the top and a relatively flat body.

With high density (150 g/cm²), the cloth is much heavier, causing it to sag dramatically, the top edge curves deeply downward and the sides pull inward significantly, producing a shape visually similar to the low-ks case since both result in greater deformation relative to the spring forces.

Effects of Changing Damping

With damping at 0%, energy is never removed from the system. As seen in the damping0 image, the cloth continues oscillating and rippling, visible as wave-like folds across the upper surface, and never reaches a true resting state.

With damping at ~1% (damping1), the cloth loses energy quickly and settles into a smooth, stable resting position with no oscillation, falling in a slow controlled motion compared to the undamped case.
